# Rules for Writing the Methodology (Metodologia / Procedimentos Metodológicos)

## Purpose
This chapter details all instruments and methodological procedures used by the researcher to carry out the research. Every methodological choice must be: named, applied to the specific research context, and justified by at least one theoretical source from the methodology field.

## The Three Mandatory Elements for Each Methodological Choice
For every decision made in this chapter, the text must present:
1. **The choice** — what was selected (e.g., "qualitative approach")
2. **How it applies** — how this choice fits the specific research context
3. **The source** — at least one author's definition or justification for the choice

## Required Sections

### 3.1 Research Nature
- **Basic** (theoretical advancement) or **Applied** (practical problem-solving)

### 3.2 Research Characterization
- **Approach**: Qualitative and/or Quantitative
- **Perspective**: Transversal (snapshot in time) or Longitudinal (over time); specify the time period studied

### 3.3 Research Classification
- **By Ends** (purpose): Descriptive, Exploratory, Explanatory, Applied, Methodological, Interventionist
  - Descriptive is the most common for undergraduate-level TCCs
- **By Means** (type): Field research, Documental, Bibliographic, Case Study, Experimental, Ex-post facto, Participant, Action Research

### 3.4 Research Delimitation
- **Population/Universe**: Who or what is being studied? Define the total group of elements with characteristics relevant to the research objectives (can be people, organizations, departments, processes, etc.)
- **Sample**: The representative subset of the population. May be:
  - **Probabilistic** (statistically determined quantity and selection)
  - **Non-probabilistic** (intentional selection by accessibility and/or typicality)
  - **Census** (all elements of the population included)
- **Research Subjects**: The individuals who will provide data (sometimes the same as the population)

### 3.5 Data Collection Techniques and Instruments
**Specify both primary and secondary data sources:**

#### Primary Data Instruments
- Structured observation
- Questionnaire (written questions answered in writing; can be open, semi-structured, or closed)
- Form (researcher fills in as respondent answers orally)
- Semi-structured interview (uses a guide/script)
- Unstructured interview (free conversation)
- Focus group
- Diaries / Life histories

#### Secondary Data Sources
- Internal: reports, invoices, financial records, databases
- External: books, academic papers, articles, statistical data, journals

**For each instrument, specify:**
- What instrument was used and why
- How it was constructed (source of questions)
- Number of questions and their purpose
- Whether a consent letter was used
- Whether a pilot test (pre-test) was applied and what adjustments resulted

**Note**: Using a previously validated instrument from another study is acceptable and encouraged. Cite the source and justify the choice.

### 3.6 Data Analysis Techniques
- **Quantitative data**: statistical tools (e.g., standard deviation, frequency, mean, mode, T-student, Chi-square)
- **Qualitative data**: interpretive models (e.g., Triviños model, Pattern Matching, Content Analysis)

### 3.7 Limitations of the Method
This section defines the **scope boundaries** of the research — NOT personal difficulties faced by the researcher.

Four required scope dimensions:
- **Temporal**: Specify the time period of the research (e.g., "This research is limited to the months of January through May 2024")
- **Theoretical**: Specify which theories/concepts were used to frame the research and why (e.g., "This study adopts the socio-technical concept of Campos Filho (1999)")
- **Geographic**: Specify the geographic/organizational context (e.g., "Data obtained in this study apply only to Organization X and cannot be generalized to other organizations")
- **Technical**: Specify the statistical or analytical tools used for data treatment

## Tip for Articles (Limited Pages)
Since articles have page limits, you may present all methodology content in a single unified text instead of numbered subsections — but all elements (3.1 through 3.7) must still be present.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Do NOT describe personal difficulties as "limitations" (e.g., "the respondent didn't have time")
- Do NOT forget to justify each methodological choice with a theoretical source
- Do NOT omit how the instrument was constructed or validated
- Do NOT confuse population with sample
- Do NOT present the methodology without connecting it to the research objectives

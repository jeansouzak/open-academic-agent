# Rules for Writing the Theoretical-Empirical Revision (Revisão da Literatura / Fundamentação Teórica)

## Purpose
This chapter provides the theoretical foundation that supports the research. It gives the conceptual basis for analyzing and interpreting the collected data, and also guides the construction of data collection instruments.

## What This Chapter Does
- Establishes the theoretical pillars of the research
- Supports the analysis performed in Chapter 4 (Data Presentation and Analysis)
- Guides the construction of the data collection instrument (questionnaire, interview guide, etc.)
- Demonstrates academic rigor and scientific credibility

## How to Identify the Topics
The topics (theoretical pillars) must correspond directly to the specific objectives and research question. Ask: "What theoretical concepts do I need to understand to answer my research question?"

**Example**: If the general objective is "Analyze the use of security systems related to intranet access in Organization X", the theoretical pillars would be:
- 2.1 Intranet — concepts, uses, and functions
- 2.2 Security policies and systems in IT, focusing on intranet
- 2.3 Human resistance to technological innovation

## Required Content Rules

### Depth and Critical Engagement
- Do NOT simply summarize or list authors' ideas
- You must **analyze and confront** different theoretical positions
- Point out gaps, weak points, or non-discussed aspects in existing literature
- Express agreement or disagreement with conclusions, always with justification
- Argumentation must be built with **depth, coherence, clarity, and elegance**

### Use of Sources
- Cite current and classic (foundational) sources from the field
- Sources can come from: Internet, articles, journals, books, monographs, theses, dissertations, laws, company documents
- Only include theory that is directly useful to your research — avoid "filling pages"
- Plagiarism is a crime and devalues the work — always cite sources properly

### Citation Rules (per ABNT NBR 10520/2002)

#### Direct Quote — up to 3 lines
- Placed within quotation marks in the running text
- Include: Author (last name), year, page number
- Example: According to Silva (2020, p. 31), "the system needs continuous updates."

#### Direct Quote — more than 3 lines
- No quotation marks
- Font size 10, single spacing, 4 cm left indent
- Include: Author (last name), year, page number

#### Indirect Quote (Paraphrase)
- Interpret or rephrase the author's idea in your own words
- Include: Author (last name) and year (NO page number)
- The author's name can appear at the beginning, middle, or end of the paragraph
- Avoid repeating the same pattern (author at the start) too many times in sequence

#### Citation of Citation (apud)
- Used only when the original source was not accessed
- Format: Original Author (year apud CITING AUTHOR, year)
- Example: Skinner (1975 apud FRANÇA, 2007) states that...
- Always try to find and cite the original source when possible

## Structure
```
2 LITERATURE REVIEW (or THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK)

2.1 [First theoretical pillar — derived from specific objective 1]

2.2 [Second theoretical pillar — derived from specific objective 2]

2.3 [Third theoretical pillar — derived from specific objective 3]
```

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Do NOT include a theoretical concept that is not used in the data analysis
- Do NOT reproduce long author summaries without critical engagement
- Do NOT ignore opposing theoretical positions
- Do NOT forget proper citations for all referenced ideas
- Do NOT write this chapter as a "filler" — every section must have research utility
